    Weather Learning Guide

    This page explains what each reading means and how to use it in daily decisions.

    Contents

    AQI Bands Wind Guide Radar Legend Map Reading Humidity & Dew Point UV Risk Alert Terms Winter Types Risk Profiles Activity Matrix Lightning Safety Flood Triggers Forecast Confidence FAQ Emergency Checklist Glossary

    Temperature

    Air temperature measured about 2 meters above ground. Use it for clothing and comfort.

    Feels Like

    Adjusted temperature that includes wind chill (cold) or heat index (hot/humid).

    Wind Speed & Direction

    Speed is how fast air moves. Direction is where wind comes from (for example, West means from the west).

    Wind Gusts

    Short bursts stronger than average wind. Important for driving, biking, and outdoor safety.

    Humidity

    How much moisture is in the air. Higher humidity can make warm weather feel hotter.

    Dew Point

    Temperature at which air becomes saturated. Higher dew point means a stickier, more humid feel.

    Pressure

    Atmospheric pressure in hPa. Falling pressure can signal unsettled weather.

    Visibility

    How far you can clearly see. Low visibility often occurs with fog, heavy rain, or snow.

    Cloud Cover

    Percent of sky covered by clouds. 0% is clear; 100% is overcast.

    Radar

    Shows precipitation intensity and motion. Use animation to see where rain/snow is moving.

    AQI (Air Quality Index)

    Higher AQI means worse air. Sensitive groups should reduce outdoor activity when AQI is elevated.

    Sunrise / Sunset / Moon

    Useful for planning daylight hours, photography, and evening conditions.

    AQI Severity Bands (US AQI)

    Higher values mean more pollution risk, especially for sensitive groups.

    0-50 Good Air is clean for most people.
    51-100 Moderate Unusually sensitive people may notice symptoms.
    101-150 Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups Children, elderly, and respiratory patients should limit exertion.
    151-200 Unhealthy Everyone may begin to feel effects; reduce prolonged outdoor activity.
    201-300 Very Unhealthy Health alert conditions; stay indoors when possible.
    301+ Hazardous Emergency conditions; avoid outdoor exposure.

    Wind Threshold Guide

    Use sustained wind for general conditions and gusts for sudden impacts.

    0-7 mph Calm to Light Little effect on activities.
    8-18 mph Breezy Noticeable wind; light debris may move.
    19-31 mph Windy Harder for high-profile vehicles and cyclists.
    32-46 mph Strong Tree limbs can break; caution outdoors.
    47+ mph Severe / Damaging Potential structural impacts and power interruptions.

    Radar Intensity Legend (General)

    Color shades generally map to precipitation intensity from light to extreme.

    Light Blue / Green Light Rain or Snow Usually low impact, but can reduce visibility.
    Yellow Moderate Precipitation Steadier rain/snow, pooling possible in poor drainage.
    Orange / Red Heavy Rain / Strong Cells Rapid visibility drop and localized flooding risk.
    Dark Red / Purple Very Intense Storm Core Severe weather possible; monitor warnings closely.

    How To Read The Map Quickly

    • Start with radar animation direction to see where precipitation is moving next 30-60 minutes.
    • Check wind arrows for transport direction of smoke, storms, and temperature changes.
    • Compare AQI and wind together: poor AQI plus low wind can mean slower air cleanup.
    • Use sunrise/sunset values to plan travel and outdoor time before dark.
    • Treat alerts as highest priority when present, even if current radar looks quiet.

    Humidity & Dew Point Comfort Scale

    Dew point is often the best quick signal for how sticky the air will feel.

    Below 50 F Dry / Comfortable Most people feel comfortable, especially in shade.
    50-59 F Pleasant Slight humidity, still comfortable for most activity.
    60-64 F Sticky Many people begin to notice muggy conditions.
    65-69 F Very Humid Outdoor exertion can feel much harder.
    70 F+ Oppressive / Tropical Heat stress risk rises quickly; hydrate and take breaks.

    Pressure Trend Clues

    The trend matters more than a single pressure number.

    • Rising pressure usually signals stabilizing weather and improving conditions.
    • Falling pressure often signals approaching storms, fronts, or unsettled weather.
    • A rapid pressure drop can be a warning sign for stronger systems.
    • Compare pressure changes with wind shifts and radar movement for better timing.

    Visibility Guide

    6+ mi Good Normal driving and outdoor visibility.
    3-6 mi Fair Haze/light precipitation may reduce distant detail.
    1-3 mi Poor Use caution while driving; increase following distance.
    Below 1 mi Dangerous Fog/heavy precip can make travel hazardous.

    UV Index Risk Bands

    0-2 Low Minimal protection needed for most people.
    3-5 Moderate Use sunscreen and sunglasses.
    6-7 High Limit midday sun and protect skin.
    8-10 Very High Burn risk rises quickly; seek shade often.
    11+ Extreme Unprotected skin can burn rapidly.

    Alert Terms: Watch vs Warning vs Advisory

    Term Meaning What To Do
    Watch Conditions are favorable. Prepare and monitor updates.
    Warning Dangerous weather is happening or imminent. Take action now to protect life and property.
    Advisory Hazardous conditions likely to cause inconvenience or minor impacts. Use extra caution.

    Precipitation Probability Basics

    • A 30% rain chance means a 3-in-10 chance of measurable precipitation at your location.
    • Probability does not equal duration: short heavy bursts can happen with moderate percentages.
    • Use radar animation with hourly probability for best near-term planning.
    • Snow type and accumulation depend strongly on near-surface temperature and layer depth aloft.

    Reading Cloud Cover

    0-20% Mostly Clear Good sky visibility and strong daytime sun.
    21-60% Partly Cloudy Variable sun with passing clouds.
    61-85% Mostly Cloudy Limited sunshine and cooler daytime heating.
    86-100% Overcast Gray skies; often linked with steady precipitation systems.

    Severe Weather Quick Signals

    • Rapidly strengthening radar cores plus rising winds can indicate stronger storm potential.
    • Sudden wind direction shifts near a storm can mark boundary passage or outflow.
    • Frequent lightning, intense rain rates, and repeated cells over one area raise flash-flood concern.
    • Always prioritize official warnings over visual interpretation.

    Unit Conversion Quick Reference

    Metric US Rule of Thumb
    C F 20C is about 68F, 30C is about 86F.
    km/h mph mph is roughly km/h divided by 1.6.
    mm in 25 mm is about 1 inch.
    km mi 1 mile is about 1.6 km.

    Data Timing Notes

    • Current values and hourly forecasts can update at different cadences depending on source.
    • Radar frames can arrive every few minutes and may have occasional provider delays.
    • A single outlier value should be interpreted with recent trend context when possible.

    Personal Risk Profiles

    These groups should use tighter limits when planning outdoor time.

    Profile Watch Closely Suggested Action
    Children Heat index, AQI, lightning Shorten play in heat/haze and move inside at thunder.
    Older Adults Heat, wind chill, poor AQI Avoid peak heat/cold windows and reduce exertion.
    Asthma/COPD AQI, smoke transport wind Limit outdoor effort when AQI rises above 100.
    Outdoor Workers Heat stress, lightning, strong gusts Use hydration/rest cycles and stop work for nearby storms.
    Pets Hot pavement, cold wind, AQI Shorten walks in extreme heat/cold and poor air.

    Activity Decision Matrix

    Use this quick matrix to decide if activity is generally good, caution, or postpone.

    Activity Caution Triggers Postpone Triggers
    Run / Bike AQI 101+, gusts 25+ mph, heat index above 90 F Lightning nearby, AQI 151+, visibility below 1 mile
    Yard Work Heat index 95+, sustained wind 25+ mph Warning-level storms or severe gust risk
    Commuting Visibility 1-3 miles, heavy radar returns Flooded roads, freezing rain, visibility below 1 mile
    Grilling / Events Gusts 20+ mph, rain cores approaching Lightning or severe thunderstorm warnings
    Long Drive Strong crosswinds and moderate heavy precip Ice accretion risk, flash-flood warnings, blizzard-like visibility

    Lightning Safety Rules

    • 30/30 rule: if thunder follows lightning within 30 seconds, move indoors immediately.
    • Wait at least 30 minutes after the last thunder before resuming outdoor activity.
    • Safe shelter means a fully enclosed building or hard-top vehicle with windows closed.
    • Avoid trees, open fields, metal fences, and water during thunderstorms.
    • If indoors, avoid corded electronics and plumbing during active lightning.

    Flood and Heavy Rain Triggers

    • Slow-moving radar cores repeatedly passing over one area can cause flash flooding.
    • Urban areas flood faster due to hard surfaces and poor drainage.
    • Rising stream or ditch levels plus continued heavy rain indicate escalating risk.
    • Never drive through water-covered roads; depth and road damage are hard to judge.
    • Turn around, do not drown: the safest route is always to avoid flooded crossings.

    Winter Precipitation Type Guide

    Type Typical Setup Common Impact
    Snow Colder layers top to bottom Accumulation, lower visibility, slower travel
    Sleet Melting aloft then refreezing before ground Pellet ice and slick roads
    Freezing Rain Warm layer aloft with shallow subfreezing surface High-impact glaze ice on roads, trees, and power lines
    Cold Rain Surface above freezing Wet roads without ice unless temps drop later

    Forecast Confidence Explained

    High Confidence

    Models agree on timing and intensity. Day planning is usually stable.

    Medium Confidence

    General pattern is clear, but timing or totals may shift.

    Low Confidence

    Competing scenarios. Recheck often and plan with larger safety margins.

    Local Climate Normals Context

    • Compare today to normal seasonal values, not only yesterday.
    • A temperature departure of plus/minus 10 F from normal is usually notable.
    • Several wet or dry weeks in a row matter more than one unusual day.
    • Use normals to judge whether current patterns are typical for your area and month.

    FAQ

    Why does radar show rain but I do not feel it yet?

    Precipitation can evaporate before reaching the ground, or the core may pass just north/south of your exact location.

    Why did temperature change quickly in one hour?

    A front passage, cloud break, rain-cooled outflow, or wind direction shift can change temperature rapidly.

    Why is wind gust much higher than sustained wind?

    Sustained wind is an average over time, while gusts capture brief stronger bursts.

    Why can AQI be poor even when sky looks clear?

    Fine particles and ozone are not always visible. Transport winds can carry pollution long distances.

    Why does forecast rain timing keep shifting?

    Small speed changes in fronts or storm lines can move arrival by hours, especially in warm-season convection.

    Why can one neighborhood get heavy rain while another stays dry?

    Thunderstorms are highly localized. A slight shift in storm track can create large differences over short distances.

    Why does it feel hotter than the listed air temperature?

    High humidity reduces evaporative cooling from skin. Direct sun and low wind can make it feel even warmer.

    Why is the app showing no precipitation while radar still has color?

    Radar reflectivity may detect virga, non-reaching snow grains, or weak echoes that do not produce measurable ground accumulation.

    What is the difference between weather watch and warning again?

    A watch means conditions are favorable. A warning means hazardous weather is occurring or imminent and action should be taken now.

    How often should I refresh during severe weather?

    Check every few minutes when storms are nearby. Radar and alerts can update quickly as conditions evolve.

    Why does wind direction matter for storms and smoke?

    Wind direction controls where storm outflow, smoke, and pollution plumes travel, affecting local impact timing.

    How can I tell if roads might flood soon?

    Look for repeated heavy radar cells over the same area, poor drainage zones, and active flood alerts.

    Why does freezing rain cause more damage than snow?

    Freezing rain forms glaze ice on roads, trees, and power lines, creating severe traction and infrastructure risk.

    Can good visibility still be dangerous for driving?

    Yes. Wind gusts, black ice, or sudden hydroplaning can create hazards even when visibility appears acceptable.

    What should I trust most when map layers disagree?

    Prioritize official alerts first, then current observations/radar, then short-term forecast trends.

    Why does sunrise or sunset time matter beyond daylight?

    Temperature, visibility, and road conditions can change quickly around sunrise/sunset, and glare risk increases.

    How should I plan outdoor exercise using this app?

    Check AQI, temperature/feels-like, wind gusts, and radar trend together. Avoid peak heat, smoke, and nearby lightning.

    Emergency Readiness Checklist

    • Water, food, medications, flashlight, and batteries for at least 72 hours.
    • Phone power bank charged before severe weather windows.
    • Family meeting point and communication plan.
    • Weather radio or alert-enabled phone notifications.
    • Vehicle fuel and alternate route plan for flood or ice risk.
    • Pet food, leash, and carrier ready for rapid relocation.

    Glossary Quick Terms

    Dew Point

    Temperature where air saturates; stronger comfort indicator than humidity percent.

    Heat Index

    How hot it feels when humidity is combined with air temperature.

    Wind Chill

    How cold it feels when wind increases heat loss from skin.

    Reflectivity

    Radar measure of returned energy; higher values often indicate heavier precipitation.

    Advection

    Horizontal movement of air that transports heat, moisture, smoke, or haze.

    Inversion

    Layer where temperature rises with height, often trapping pollution near the surface.

    Outflow Boundary

    Cool air spreading from storms that can trigger new storm development.

    Nowcasting

    Very short-term forecasting focused on the next minutes to few hours.
